LECTURE 5 — TEACHER SUMMARY
Interactive Design 4 | NABA Ludocore


TEACHING OBJECTIVES

The central ambition of this session was to elevate the students' understanding of interaction from the mechanical ("put a trigger zone here, wire an event there") to the structural and expressive. The instructor wanted them to internalize a tripartite model — input, transformation, output — not as abstract theory but as a practical thinking tool that makes any interaction designable. Beneath this lay a quieter goal: to connect technical implementation to emotional intentionality. The Backrooms aesthetic was chosen deliberately as a proving ground where every design decision — a reverb curve, a light delay, a pitch ramp — could be evaluated against a target feeling: liminality, uncanniness, atmospheric unease. The instructor was also scaffolding a workflow discipline around AI-assisted development, reinforcing the separation of specification from implementation and of brainstorming from building.


PEDAGOGICAL APPROACHES

The session moved through three distinct pedagogical modes. It opened with conceptual framing — slides, a vocabulary of signal types (discrete vs. continuous), and a 2x2 matrix of interaction categories (one-shot, switch, binding, conditional). This was kept brisk and referential, pointing back to what students already knew from previous semesters. The second mode was tool demonstration: the instructor walked students through a procedural room generator he had built, showing how to populate a scriptable object with tile segments and generate maze-like environments. This was deliberately pragmatic — he wanted to remove level design as a bottleneck so students could focus on interaction design rather than spending entire sessions placing walls by hand. The third mode was live, iterative prototyping: writing a specification document, feeding it to an AI agent, testing the generated component in Unity, then revising the spec and regenerating. Throughout, the instructor paused frequently to check comprehension, troubleshoot individual setups (one student had no functioning computer and was loaned a laptop mid-session), and invite students to try things themselves before showing solutions.


DESIGN PHILOSOPHY AND PRINCIPLES

Several convictions surfaced repeatedly. First, that interaction design is fundamentally about the transformation step — the mapping between input and output — and that a linear mapping is almost never the interesting choice. Curves matter. The shape of how a value arrives at its destination (abruptly, gently, with overshoot) carries aesthetic and emotional weight. Second, that feedback should be layered: sound, light, material emission, camera behavior are not separate interactions but coordinated voices in a single moment, potentially staggered with delays to create texture. Third, that good interactive work is emotionally directional — it aims at something, and every implementation decision should be tested against that aim. The Backrooms served as a case study in this principle: does this reverb curve make the space feel more uncanny, or is it just noise? Finally, the instructor holds a strong architectural conviction about separating data, simulation, and presentation in code — a pattern he has codified in style guides that the AI agents also follow.


KEY IDEAS AND CONCEPTS INTRODUCED

The session formalized the "atom of interaction" as input-transformation-output, a model traced back to cybernetics but applied here to Unity interaction design. Within this, the instructor introduced a 2x2 classification: signal type (discrete or continuous) crossed with relationship type (bound or unbound), yielding four categories — one-shot triggers, switches, continuous bindings, and conditional triggers. The concept of tweening curves (via DOTween) was presented as the essential tool for giving animated life to otherwise binary interactions. Students learned that even a simple on/off trigger becomes expressive when its effect unfolds along a shaped curve over a defined duration. Audio effects built into Unity — Doppler, low-pass filter, high-pass filter, reverb, chorus, distortion — were surveyed as available output variables. The Cinemachine noise and impulse listener components were introduced as ways to bring the camera itself into the feedback layer. The instructor also introduced the workflow of writing interaction specifications in a separate text document, then handing that document to an AI coding agent for implementation — keeping design intent and code generation cleanly separated.


DIGRESSIONS AND ANECDOTES WORTH PRESERVING

The instructor mentioned he is testing a pipeline connecting a mini MIDI controller through TouchDesigner into Unity, which he plans to demonstrate next session. This is significant because it concretizes the input-transformation-output model: the same output behaviors (light, sound, material changes) can be driven by a physical controller instead of avatar proximity, proving the modularity of the framework. He also referenced "Liminal Core" and the pool rooms variant of the Backrooms as exemplary projects that achieve emotional impact almost entirely through perceptual properties — sound, light, materials — without relying on horror tropes like monsters. This was used to validate the idea that interaction design alone, without narrative crutch, can carry emotional weight. A brief aside about a student's Mac struggling with Unity led to a candid acknowledgment that game engines are fundamentally Windows-oriented tools, with Mac support being an afterthought.


STUDENT LANDSCAPE

The group appears to be a small class, comfortable enough with the instructor for informal exchanges. They have prior experience with Unity from the previous semester (trigger zones, basic events, the ecosystem simulation) and some familiarity with TouchDesigner and Max/MSP from other courses. At least one student had significant technical difficulties (non-functioning computer, needed to borrow a laptop from the media lab). The students seem receptive but not yet in the habit of practicing between sessions — the instructor noted this directly and designed the in-class exercises partly to compensate. One student suggested camera shake as an additional feedback layer, which the instructor seized on enthusiastically, suggesting the group is capable of contributing design ideas when prompted.


SESSION ARC

The session opened with a brief recap of the previous lecture's proximity sensor work, then moved quickly into the conceptual framework (the interaction atom, the 2x2 matrix). This theoretical framing lasted roughly ten minutes before shifting to the practical: importing asset packs, configuring the procedural Backrooms generator, and getting characters navigable inside generated rooms. This setup phase — necessarily mechanical — consumed perhaps twenty minutes but served the larger purpose of giving everyone a shared environment to work in. The core of the session was the live construction of a room entry interaction: a trigger that, upon the player's entrance, ramps the audio pitch from zero to one along a tweening curve, then layers in ambient lighting and emissive materials with individual delays. The instructor wrote the spec, generated code via AI, tested, revised, and regenerated — modeling the iterative workflow he wants students to adopt. The session closed with a recap of the interaction categories, a preview of upcoming topics (MIDI controllers, NPC animation, narrative text as 3D objects), and guidance on the final project's technical expectations.


FORWARD THREADS

The MIDI controller pipeline (mini controller to TouchDesigner to Unity) was explicitly promised for the next session. The instructor hinted at future coverage of physics integration, NPC movement with humanoid characters, expanded animation work, and narrative techniques using 3D text objects in the scene. The "rooms and corridors" generation mode was flagged as untested and may need refinement. Students were assigned to create at least one new interaction in the Backrooms environment before next class — either a curve-animated trigger or a continuous proximity-based mapping — and encouraged to begin thinking about their final project themes and emotional directions. The final project requirements were outlined informally: it must demonstrate multiple types of interactions used coherently toward an expressive goal, with full freedom on theme but a technical expectation of the interaction design framework taught this semester.
