# Why Agency as Art? — The Philosophy of Interactive Art

## The Deepest Question: How Does Experience Arise?

Before we can talk about interactive *art*, we need to understand why interaction matters at all — not as a technique, but as something fundamental about how subjective experience works.

Recent psychology and cognitive science have converged on a radical insight: **experience is not something that happens to us — it is something we enact.** Contemporary cognitive science describes this through what are sometimes called the **4E's of cognition** — four dimensions of how the mind actually works:

- **Embedded:** Cognition doesn't happen in a vacuum. It is situated in an environment — shaped by context, tools, spaces, and social structures. The mind is always *somewhere*, and that somewhere matters.
- **Embodied:** Cognition is not a disembodied computation. It runs through the body — through muscles, senses, posture, movement. The body is not a vehicle for the brain; it is part of the thinking itself.
- **Extended:** Cognition reaches beyond the skull. We think with notebooks, with maps, with instruments, with other people. The boundary between mind and world is porous.
- **Enacted:** This is the deepest level, and the most important for this course. Cognition is not a passive reception of information — it is an *activity*. Experience arises through the feedback loops of action, prediction, and sensory response. We do not receive the world; we *enact* it.

The enacted dimension is crucial because it reveals something fundamental: **without action, there is no sensation. Without sensation, there is no experience.** Even the seemingly passive act of looking at something involves prediction, anticipation, and micro-adjustment. Perception is not recording — it is active navigation. The mind constantly generates expectations about what will happen next, acts on those expectations, and updates itself based on the difference between prediction and reality. This is the framework of **predictive processing** and **active inference** — the mind as prediction engine, action as hypothesis-testing.

This matters enormously for what we're doing in this course, because it means: **action is not a secondary feature of experience. Action is where experience lives.** And this is why interactive art is not just another medium alongside painting, film, or music — it is a challenge to those media, an exploration of a channel that remains vastly underexplored. If experience is fundamentally enacted, then designing action is designing experience at its source. The potential of this medium — the potential of interactivity as an expressive tool — has barely been touched.

## The Question of Consciousness: What Is It Like to Be?

Thomas Nagel's 1974 paper "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" poses the foundational question of phenomenology — the study of subjective experience. Nagel's argument: a bat perceives the world through echolocation. We can describe echolocation scientifically, model it computationally, simulate it visually. But none of that captures *what it feels like* to be a bat — to navigate the world through sound, to experience space as a pattern of echoes. Consciousness, Nagel argues, is irreducible. There is always a "what it is like" that cannot be fully translated into third-person description.

This is precisely the territory of interactive art.

When we design an interactive experience, we are not designing visuals, sounds, or environments. We are designing **what it feels like to be a specific kind of agent** — a body that moves through a world, does things, encounters responses. The participant doesn't watch someone else's experience. They *have* an experience. And that experience is constituted by what they do, what the world does back, and the feedback loop between the two.

This is the link between phenomenology and interaction design: **if consciousness is fundamentally tied to action and embodiment, then designing action is designing experience at the deepest possible level.**

## What All Art Does — and What Interactive Art Does Differently

What is art for? This is a question that makes people uncomfortable, but it has a straightforward answer at the core: **art creates experience.** It moves you, transports you, changes something in you. Painting does this through vision. Music does this through time and sound. Literature does this through language and imagination. Film does this through narrative sequence.

All of these are powerful. But they share a common constraint: the audience *observes*. Even when the experience is intense — when a novel makes you weep, when a film leaves you shaken — it arrives through a fundamentally observational channel. You watch, you listen, you read. Your mind is active, certainly — mental imagery, identification, emotional resonance — but your body, your agency, your ability to *do something* and see what happens, is not part of the equation.

Interactive art removes this constraint.

**Interactive art is the art form of action.** It doesn't just show you something — it puts you inside a structure where your actions produce experience directly. The feedback loop between what you do and what happens back is the medium itself.

This is what makes it radically different from every other art form:

**Object art** (painting, sculpture) creates an artifact the audience contemplates.
**Process art** (performance, music) creates an event that unfolds in time.
**Interactive art** creates a *structure of agency* that the participant inhabits — a temporary mode of being where their actions, choices, and responses generate the experience from the inside.

## Interactive Art as Radical Empathy

If interactive art designs what it feels like to be a particular agent, then it is — at its deepest — **a medium of radical empathy.**

Not empathy in the sentimental sense (feeling sorry for someone), but empathy in the phenomenological sense: *what is it like to be this?* What is it like to be a body that moves through this space, that can do these things and not those things, that encounters these responses? What is it like to be an agent under these specific conditions?

We are so habituated to thinking through observational media — watching, reading, listening — that we forget how much richer the action-channel is. When you actually *enact* a movement, make a choice, feel the resistance of a constraint, experience the consequences of your action — the understanding that arises is qualitatively different from understanding through observation. It is *first-person* understanding. It is knowing-by-doing.

This is why interactive art can create experiences that no other medium can reach:

- **Papers, Please** doesn't tell you that bureaucracy dehumanizes — it makes you *perform* the dehumanization, stamp by stamp, and feel the moral weight in your hands.
- **September 12th** doesn't argue that military intervention creates more enemies — it makes you *enact* the cycle and discover the argument through your own actions.
- **Journey** doesn't describe loneliness and connection — it puts you in a desert with an anonymous stranger and lets the *constraint* of wordless communication generate the emotion.

None of these experiences can be replaced by a video of someone else doing them. The meaning lives in the *doing*.

## Nguyen's Framework: The Mechanics of Designed Agency

C. Thi Nguyen, in *Games: Agency as Art* (2020), provides the structural framework for understanding how agency is designed. His core claim:

**Games are the art form whose medium is human agency itself.**

The designer specifies three things:
- **Goals** — what the participant is supposed to care about (temporarily)
- **Abilities** — what the participant can do
- **Constraints** — what the participant cannot do

Together, these construct a **temporary agency** — a mode of being that the participant inhabits for the duration of the experience. The designer doesn't control what the participant does; they sculpt the *space of possible action*, and the participant navigates it.

Key insights from Nguyen that apply directly to this course:

**Crystallization.** Aesthetic experiences of action happen naturally in life — the grace of dodging an obstacle, the satisfaction of solving a problem, the anguish of an impossible choice. But they are rare and uncontrolled. Interactive art *concentrates* these experiences. It engineers the conditions for specific qualities of action to arise.

**Process art.** The artwork is not the artifact (the game, the installation, the code). The artwork is **the actions the artifact produces in the participant.** Evaluation should focus not on how something looks, but on what it makes people *do* and what that doing *feels like*.

**A library of agencies.** Each interactive work crystallizes a different mode of agency — a different way of being an agent in a world. A collection of works becomes a library of different ways of acting, choosing, and experiencing. By the end of this course, the class will have built its own small library.

**Value clarity and value capture.** Interactive works can offer seductive clarity — crisp goals, clear feedback, measurable success. This is powerful as a design tool, but dangerous as an ideology. When simplified metrics replace rich experience, meaning collapses. The designer must understand this tension.

## Campbell's Answer: What Is All This For?

Nguyen tells us *what* interactive art does (sculpts agency) and *how* (goals, abilities, constraints). But he stops short of the deeper question: **what is it all for?** Why does it matter that someone experiences a moment of grace while navigating a designed interaction?

Joseph Campbell, drawing on Joyce, Aquinas, and Vedantic philosophy, provides the answer:

**The purpose of art is epiphany — the moment where the surface of experience becomes transparent and something essential shines through.**

Joyce called it **radiance**. Aquinas called it **quidditas** — the "whatness" of a thing. Campbell calls it the moment where meaning doesn't need to be explained, because it is *directly experienced*.

In interactive art, this epiphany arrives through action. When the design works — truly works — the participant doesn't just "do something." They experience a moment where their own action becomes meaningful beyond itself. The doing becomes transparent. Something shines through.

This is what Csikszentmihalyi calls **flow**, what Nguyen calls **harmony of capacity**, and what Campbell calls **eternity in the present moment**. They all point at the same thing: **the collapse of distance between the person and what they're doing.**

## The Enactive Synthesis: Putting It All Together

The philosophical foundation of this course draws three streams together:

**From enactivism and phenomenology:** Experience arises through action. Consciousness is embodied, enacted, constituted by the feedback loops of doing and sensing. Designing action is designing experience at the most fundamental level.

**From Nguyen:** Agency is a designable medium. Goals, abilities, and constraints construct temporary modes of being. Interactive works crystallize specific qualities of action into shareable, repeatable experiences.

**From Campbell:** The purpose is epiphany through action — meaning that shines through doing, not layered on top. The aesthetic endgame is not entertainment, not engagement, not "interactivity" as a feature. It is the moment where the participant *becomes* what they are doing, and something essential is revealed.

## Actions as Anchors, Not Metaphors

A critical distinction: in interactive art, actions are not *metaphors* for experience. They are **anchors** for experience.

A metaphor says: "this thing is like that thing." It works through analogy, through distance. You read about loneliness, and it reminds you of loneliness you've felt.

An anchor says: "you are here, doing this, now." It works through direct enactment. You walk toward a figure and it dissolves as you approach. You don't *think about* loneliness — you enact the impossibility of connection, and the feeling arises from the doing.

This is the shift from observational art to enacted art. The experience doesn't arrive through representation. It arrives through participation.

## The Irreducibility Principle

The central critical test of this course:

> **Could this experience be replaced by a video?**
> If someone watching a recording would get essentially the same experience as someone participating — the interaction is not yet doing its work.

This is not a binary test — it's a compass. It asks: *where does the meaning live?* If the meaning lives in the visuals, the narrative, the atmosphere — those are fine things, but they are observational art. If the meaning lives in *what the participant does* and in *what that doing feels like* — that is interactive art.

The question we will ask about everything we create:

> **What does it feel like to be this agent? What does this doing produce in the person who does it?**

---

## For Every Session, For Every Project

This philosophy is not a separate lecture topic — it is the lens applied to everything. For every reference artwork we analyze, for every technique we learn, for every project we build and critique, the question remains:

**How does it feel to be the person doing this?**

Not: how does it look? Not: what story does it tell? Not: is the technology impressive?

**What does the doing feel like, and what does it produce in the one who does it?**
