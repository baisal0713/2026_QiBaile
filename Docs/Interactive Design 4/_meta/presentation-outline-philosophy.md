## SLIDE 2 — The Question

**What is art for?**

*(Just the question. Let it sit. The discomfort is the point. Speaker opens a conversation before revealing any framework.)*

Speaker notes: Art creates experience. It moves, transports, changes something in you. Every art form does this — but each one does it through a different *language*. Painting speaks through color and composition. Music speaks through time and harmony. We're going to ask: what language does interactive art speak? And what can that language say that no other language can?

---

## SLIDE 3 — Every Art Form Has a Language

**Painting → the language of seeing.**
**Music → the language of hearing.**
**Literature → the language of reading.**
**Film → the language of watching.**

*(Stacked text. Simple. Building toward the punchline.)*

Speaker notes: These are all enormously powerful languages. But they share something in common: the audience *observes*. Even when the experience is intense — when a novel wrecks you, when a film leaves you shaken — it arrives through an observational channel. Your mind is active, absolutely. But your body, your agency, your ability to *do something and see what happens*, is not part of the equation. Interactive art doesn't abandon what these art forms pursue. It pursues the same thing — experience, meaning, transformation — but through a fundamentally different language.

---

## SLIDE 4 — The Language of Doing

**Interactive art → the language of action.**

*(Same visual language as previous slide. The addition lands.)*

Speaker notes: Interactive art speaks through action. It doesn't show you something — it puts you inside a structure where your actions produce experience directly. The feedback loop between what you do and what happens back *is* the language. And the interaction framework — triggers, conditions, actions, variables, events — is the grammar of that language. Every design choice about what the participant can do, what the world does back, what changes and what persists — that's syntax. That's vocabulary. That's composition. The same way a painter composes with color, we compose with agency.

---

## SLIDE 5 — Why Action? (The Science)

**Experience doesn't happen to you. You enact it.**

*(Subtext or small caption: "The enactive approach to cognition")*

Speaker notes: This isn't philosophy in the abstract — it's backed by cognitive science. The enactive approach says that experience arises through feedback loops of action, prediction, and sensory response. Perception is not passive recording — it's active navigation. The brain constantly predicts what will happen next, acts on those predictions, and updates itself based on the difference. This means action is not a secondary feature of experience. Action is where experience *lives*. And that's why designing action is designing experience at its deepest source.

---

## SLIDE 6 — The 4E's of Cognition

**Embedded** — cognition is situated in an environment
**Embodied** — cognition runs through the body
**Extended** — cognition reaches beyond the skull
**Enacted** — cognition is an activity, not a reception

*(Four terms stacked. "Enacted" visually emphasized — larger, bold, or highlighted.)*

Speaker notes: Contemporary cognitive science describes cognition through these four dimensions. Embedded: we think in context, shaped by tools and spaces. Embodied: the body is not a vehicle for the brain — it's part of the thinking. Extended: we think with notebooks, maps, instruments, other people. Enacted: this is the deepest level. Cognition is not passive reception of information — it is something we *do*. Without action, there is no sensation. Without sensation, there is no experience. The enacted dimension tells us that experience is fundamentally constituted through action. This is the scientific basis for everything we'll do in this course.

## SLIDE 7 — What Is It Like to Be a Bat?

**Thomas Nagel, 1974:**
*"What is it like to be a bat?"*

*(Image of a bat using echolocation, or a simple silhouette. The question as large text.)*

Speaker notes: Nagel's famous paper. A bat perceives the world through echolocation. We can describe it scientifically, model it, simulate it. But none of that captures what it *feels like* to be a bat — to experience space as echoes. Consciousness has a "what it is like" that can't be fully translated into third-person description. This is phenomenology — the study of subjective experience. And this is exactly the territory we're entering. When we design an interactive experience, we are designing *what it feels like to be a specific kind of agent*.

---

## SLIDE 8 — The Core Claim

**We are designing what it feels like to be an agent.**

*(Central text. This is the thesis of the course. Let it breathe.)*

Speaker notes: Not what it looks like. Not what story it tells. What it *feels like* — to be a body that moves through this world, that can do these things and not those things, that encounters these responses. The question works from both directions: what actions produce a certain feeling? And what feeling do we want — what actions would get us there? This bidirectional question is at the heart of every design decision we'll make.

---

## SLIDE 9 — A Gradient, Not a Break

**Object art:** the artist makes an artifact. You contemplate it.
**Process art:** the artist creates an event. You witness it.
**Interactive art:** the artist creates a structure of agency. You inhabit it.

*(Three-tier visual. A gradient — increasing participation, not a hierarchy of value.)*

Speaker notes: This is not a ranking. Object art, process art, and interactive art all pursue the same thing: they create experience, they produce meaning, they aim for that moment where something essential is revealed. The difference is the channel. Object art speaks through contemplation. Process art speaks through witnessing. Interactive art speaks through inhabiting — through the participant's own actions inside a designed structure. The artwork is neither the artifact nor the event. It's the *actions the artifact produces in the participant*.

---

## SLIDE 10 — Nguyen's Framework

**C. Thi Nguyen — *Games: Agency as Art* (2020)**

The designer specifies:
**Goals** — what you care about (temporarily)
**Abilities** — what you can do
**Constraints** — what you cannot do

→ Together: a **temporary agency** you inhabit.

Speaker notes: Nguyen gives us the structural toolkit. Every interactive work constructs a temporary mode of being for the participant. The designer doesn't control what the participant does — they sculpt the *space of possible action*. Goals, abilities, and constraints are the materials. Just as a painter uses color, we use the structure of action to evoke experience.

---

## SLIDE 11 — Crystallization

**Aesthetic experiences of action happen naturally — but they're rare.**
**Interactive art *concentrates* them.**

*(Visual: scattered dots → focused cluster. Or: dilute → concentrated.)*

Speaker notes: The grace of dodging an obstacle. The anguish of an impossible choice. The satisfaction of a perfect solution. These moments happen in life, but they're scattered and accidental. Csikszentmihalyi calls it flow — the collapse of distance between the person and what they're doing. Some people experience this often; he calls it the autotelic personality — someone who generates intrinsic purpose from the activity itself. Interactive art is a technology for *engineering* these moments. It creates the conditions so that specific qualities of action reliably arise. When it works, the participant forgets the interface, the action and the response feel like one continuous thing, the meaning isn't explained — it's *enacted*. Each work you make this semester should crystallize one specific experience of agency.

---

## SLIDE 12 — Radiance

**Campbell / Joyce: Epiphany**
*The moment where the surface of experience becomes transparent, and something essential shines through.*

Speaker notes: Joseph Campbell, drawing on Joyce and Aquinas, gives us the word for what we're after: *radiance*. It's the moment in art where you don't need the meaning explained — you *experience it directly*. In interactive art, this epiphany arrives through action. When the design truly works, the participant's action becomes meaningful beyond itself. The doing becomes transparent. Something shines through. This is what crystallization aims at — not just engagement, not interactivity as a feature, but the moment where the participant *becomes* what they are doing.

---

## SLIDE 13 — A Library of Agencies

**Chess** → analytic calculation
**Journey** → wordless companionship
**Papers, Please** → moral bureaucracy
**Getting Over It** → persistence under futility
**Flower** → movement as restoration

*(List of works, each with its agentic mode. Could be visual with screenshots.)*

Speaker notes: Every interactive work crystallizes a different mode of agency — a different way of being an agent in a world. A collection of works becomes a library of agencies. This is critical: the richness of the medium lies in its range. Two people who never meet, separated by geography and language, can play the same game and share the same agentic experience. By the end of this course, the class will have built its own library — each of you contributing a different mode of being.

## SLIDE 14 — Radical Empathy (One Possibility)

**What is it like to *be* this?**

*Papers, Please* — what is it like to perform bureaucratic dehumanization?
*September 12th* — what is it like to enact a cycle of violence?
*Journey* — what is it like to connect without words?

Speaker notes: Not every agentic mode is about empathy — some crystallize grace, others frustration, others contemplation. But one of the most powerful things interactive art can do is produce *radical empathy*: first-person understanding of what it's like to be a particular agent under particular conditions. Not empathy in the sentimental sense, but phenomenological empathy — knowing by doing. When you actually enact a movement, make a choice, feel the resistance of a constraint — the understanding that arises is qualitatively different from watching someone else do it. This is one reason the medium matters: it can reach experiences that no observational art form can touch.

## SLIDE 15 — Actions as Anchors *(discussion point)*

**Actions are not metaphors for experience.**
**Actions are anchors for experience.**

*(Flag this slide as a provocation for class discussion.)*

Speaker notes: A metaphor says: "this is like that." It works through analogy, through distance. An anchor says: "you are here, doing this, now." It works through direct enactment. You walk toward a figure and it dissolves as you approach — you don't *think about* loneliness, you enact the impossibility of connection, and the feeling arises from the doing itself. *(Note to self: this distinction feels right but I want to discuss it with the class. Where does this idea come from? Is it phenomenology? Enactivism? Is it a useful frame or does it need refinement? Let's think about it together.)*

## SLIDE 16 — The Irreducibility Test

**Could this be a video?**

*If someone watching a recording gets the same experience as someone participating — the interaction is not yet doing its work.*

Speaker notes: This is the recurring critical question of the course. Not a binary pass/fail, but a compass. It asks: where does the meaning live? If the meaning lives in visuals, narrative, atmosphere — those are fine things, but they're observational art. If the meaning lives in what the participant *does* and what that doing *feels like* — that is interactive art. We will ask this about every reference artwork, every technique, every project.

---

## SLIDE 17 — What Good Interactive Art Feels Like

- You forget the interface
- Your action and the response feel like one continuous thing
- Meaning isn't explained — it's **enacted**
- "Oh — *this* is what loneliness feels like" (not: "this represents loneliness")

Speaker notes: These are the markers. When the design works, the technology disappears. The participant isn't "using" something — they're *being* something. And the recognition that arrives isn't intellectual ("I see what this means") — it's experiential ("I feel what this is"). This is crystallization in practice: the moment where action and meaning become the same thing.

---

## SLIDE 18 — What Bad Interactive Art Feels Like

- The interaction delivers visuals or narrative (it's a carrier, not the medium)
- You could get the same experience from watching a video
- The interface is visible — you're aware of "using" something
- Meaning is stated, not produced through action

Speaker notes: These are the warning signs. If the interaction is just a delivery mechanism for something that could have been a film or a poster — the agency isn't working yet. Go deeper. Ask: what can the participant *do* that produces meaning only through the doing?

---

## SLIDE 19 — The Question for Everything

**How does it feel to be the person doing this?**

*(This slide should feel final. This is the sentence that stays with them all semester.)*

Speaker notes: Not: how does it look? Not: is the technology impressive? Not: what does it mean? But: **how does it feel to be the person doing this?** This is the question for every reference we analyze, every technique we learn, every project we build and critique. This is the course.

## Notes on Delivery

- **Pacing:** Slides 1–6 build the foundation: what art does, what interactive art does differently, and why action matters scientifically (~8 min). Slides 7–9 connect to phenomenology and the gradient of art forms (~4 min). Slides 10–12 introduce Nguyen, crystallization, and radiance (~5 min). Slides 13–15 explore the library of agencies, radical empathy, and actions as anchors (~5 min). Slide 16 lands the irreducibility test (~2 min). Slides 17–18 give the students concrete markers for good and bad interactive art (~3 min). Slide 19 closes with the question that stays all semester.
- **Tone:** Conversational, not academic. You're provoking, not lecturing. The ideas are sophisticated, but the language should be direct.
- **Visuals:** Minimal text on slides. Large type, one idea per slide. No bullet-heavy slides. Images of reference artworks where they illustrate a point.
- **Audience:** Art/design students, not philosophy students. The enactivism and phenomenology should be gestured at, not taught formally. Nagel's bat is a vivid entry point, not a seminar topic.
- **One main idea:** Agency is art. Interaction is art. The irreducibility test. Everything else supports this.
