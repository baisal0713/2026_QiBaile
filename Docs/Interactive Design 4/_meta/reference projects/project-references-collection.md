## Interactive Installations

**Daniel Rozin — Wooden Mirror** (1999)
830 wooden tiles driven by motors reflect the visitor's silhouette in real-time.
*Does*: moves body → wood mirrors them in an alien material.
*Interest*: pure L0 reaction but the material transformation is the concept. Reinterpretation: what if the mirror distorted, delayed, or selectively forgot?

**Rafael Lozano-Hemmer — Pulse Room** (2006)
Heartbeat sensor drives a lightbulb. The bulb joins hundreds of previous visitors' recorded heartbeats.
*Does*: holds sensor → heartbeat becomes light → joins an archive of others.
*Interest*: L1 sequence + accumulation. Biometric input → persistent trace. Input design reference.

**Rafael Lozano-Hemmer — Body Movies** (2001)
Massive projectors in a public square. Your shadow, projected at enormous scale, reveals hidden photographic portraits. You must move and pose to uncover them all.
*Does*: walks, poses → shadow reveals hidden portraits that look back at you.
*Interest*: shadow as interface. Collaborative body positioning. Achievable with webcam silhouette in Unity.

**Rafael Lozano-Hemmer — Under Scan** (2005)
Portraits projected on the ground, revealed only when a visitor's shadow falls on them. The portrait makes eye contact.
*Does*: walks → shadow reveals hidden portraits.
*Interest*: L0-L1, proximity + gaze. Presence as revelation.

**Random International — Rain Room** (2012)
Rain falls everywhere except where you stand.
*Does*: walks → rain avoids them.
*Interest*: visually iconic but agency is thin (L0, binary). Better as reinterpretation: what if movement *shaped* rain rather than repelling it?

**Chris Milk — The Treachery of Sanctuary** (2012)
Three panels. Shadow transforms through phases: birds scatter, birds attack, wings grow.
*Does*: moves body through three sequential experiences.
*Interest*: L1 sequence — three phases, different agency each. Body as input. Clear emotional arc.

**Camille Utterback & Romy Achituv — Text Rain** (1999)
Letters from a poem fall like rain onto your video silhouette. Catch, hold, and play with falling text using your body.
*Does*: moves body → catches and arranges falling letters on their silhouette.
*Interest*: physics + body = readable text. Playful L0 with emergent moments. Canonical work. Very replicable in Unity.

**Golan Levin & Zach Lieberman — Messa di Voce** (2003)
Vocal sounds and body movements generate virtual objects and environments in real-time. Different vocal qualities produce different visual behaviors.
*Does*: speaks, sings, moves → voice generates visual forms.
*Interest*: voice as rich multidimensional input. Achievable with Unity microphone API.

**Memo Akten — Learning to See** (2017)
Neural networks reinterpret a live camera feed. Objects on a table become ocean waves, clouds, fire, flowers — depending on which network is active.
*Does*: arranges physical objects → sees them transformed through different "ways of seeing."
*Interest*: L2 state — same input, different outputs depending on context. "We see things as we are, not as they are." AI as subject matter.

**Joachim Sauter & Dirk Luesebrink (ART+COM) — Zerseher** (1992)
Eye-tracker follows your gaze on a classical painting. Wherever you look, the painting dissolves and decays. Looking destroys.
*Does*: looks → the painting decays where you gaze. To see, you must look away.
*Interest*: gaze as destructive force. The paradox: observation destroys the observed. L0 but conceptually rich. Buildable with mouse/cursor.

**Scott Snibbe — Boundary Functions** (1998)
Floor projection. When two people stand on it, a Voronoi line appears between them. More people = more territories. Every step restructures the diagram.
*Does*: walks → Voronoi diagram divides space, giving each person a territory.
*Interest*: social geometry. L0-L1 but emergent with multiple participants. Voronoi in Unity is achievable.

**Myron Krueger — Videoplace** (1975-1990s)
Grandfather of interactive installations. Camera captures your silhouette, projects it into a shared virtual environment. Procedural creatures respond to your movements.
*Does*: moves body → silhouette interacts with virtual objects and responsive creatures.
*Interest*: historical origin of body-as-controller. The "Critter" creatures are ancestors of modern interactive NPCs.

**teamLab — Flowers and People** (2015)
Flowers bloom and scatter around visitors. Standing still = bloom. Moving = scatter.
*Does*: moves or stays still → flowers respond to pace and presence.
*Interest*: L0, thin agency but strong aesthetic. Reinterpretation candidate: different behaviors trigger different botanical responses.

**From0** (Ars Electronica)
Visitors record words; installation fragments them into 12 pieces broadcast by pendulums. Visitors use buttons to isolate, freeze, create music loops.
*Does*: records voice → manipulates fragments → composes.
*Interest*: L1-L2, participant's input becomes the material they then sculpt.

**Robin Arnott — Soundself** (2020)
Vocalize into a microphone. Fractal/mandala visuals and binaural audio respond to and amplify your voice. System and voice form a feedback loop. Designed to induce trance.
*Does*: chants, hums, drones → system amplifies and mirrors → feedback loop deepens.
*Interest*: L4 feedback loop. Voice as meditation. The system draws you into a deeper state.

## Art Games / Experimental Games

**The Unfinished Swan** (Giant Sparrow, 2012)
Throw paint into a white void to reveal the world.
*Does*: throws paint → discovers space → the world is what you've revealed.
*Interest*: L2 state (world accumulates marks). Discovery through action.

**Beyond Eyes** (Team17, 2015)
Play as a blind girl. World renders only around you. What you imagine isn't always real.
*Does*: walks → world appears → sometimes revealed reality contradicts expectation.
*Interest*: L2-L3 (state + mismatch). Environment as perception.

**Passage** (Jason Rohrer, 2007)
5-minute game. Walk right. Age. Find a partner. Explore or proceed. Die.
*Does*: walks → micro-choices (explore or proceed) → ages → dies.
*Interest*: L1-L2. Entire life as metaphor in 5 minutes. Perfect student project scope.

**Every Day the Same Dream** (Molleindustria, 2009)
Repeat the same commute. Each run, find a deviation. Accumulated deviations transform everything.
*Does*: repeats loop → finds deviations → accumulated changes break the cycle.
*Interest*: L3-L4 (branching + feedback loop). Repetition as mechanic. Alienation through interaction.

**The Stanley Parable** (Galactic Cafe, 2013)
Narrator tells you what to do. Obey or disobey. Game responds to your rebellion.
*Does*: follows or defies narrator → game branches, comments, mocks, resets.
*Interest*: L3 branching. Agency IS the subject. Meta-interaction.

**Papers, Please** (Lucas Pope, 2013)
Border inspector. Check documents. Approve or deny. Every stamp is a moral choice.
*Does*: examines documents → makes moral judgments under pressure → lives with consequences.
*Interest*: L3-L4. UI interaction as primary mechanic. Interface-as-moral-engine.

**Nicky Case — We Become What We Behold** (2016)
Point a camera at a crowd. Photograph interesting moments. Media amplifies. Crowd changes. Loop escalates.
*Does*: frames moments → media amplifies → crowd reacts → new moments emerge → loop escalates.
*Interest*: L4 feedback loop. Player creates the conditions that change the system. Short, buildable, powerful.

**Nicky Case — Adventures with Anxiety** (2019)
You play AS the anxiety, not the person. You choose what to worry about.
*Does*: makes choices as anxiety trying to "protect" the human.
*Interest*: L3 branching. Role inversion as design. You embody the antagonist.

**Journey** (thatgamecompany, 2012)
Walk toward a mountain. Encounter anonymous strangers. Communicate only through movement and chirps.
*Does*: walks, chirps, encounters others. No dialogue, no UI.
*Interest*: L0-L1 but emotional power from stripped-down agency. Minimal communication = profound.

**Flower** (thatgamecompany, 2009)
Control wind carrying petals. Collect more. Restore dead environments.
*Does*: tilts controller → wind moves → petals collect → world transforms.
*Interest*: L1-L2. Movement-as-sensation. The interaction IS the aesthetic.

**Proteus** (Ed Key & David Kanaga, 2013)
Walk a procedural island. No goals. Everything makes music. Seasons change.
*Does*: walks → world responds sonically → time passes.
*Interest*: walking-as-composition. Landscape as musical instrument. Anti-goal design.

**Braid** (Jonathan Blow, 2008)
Platformer with time reversal. Each world has a different time mechanic.
*Does*: manipulates time differently per world → solves puzzles through temporal rules.
*Interest*: L2-L3. Each world = a different interaction model. Students could build ONE time mechanic.

**Antichamber** (Alexander Bruce, 2013)
Non-Euclidean first-person puzzles. Corridors loop, walls shift, perspective lies.
*Does*: walks, explores → space breaks the rules of logic → must abandon assumptions.
*Interest*: L2 state (rules are context-dependent). Non-Euclidean space is achievable in Unity with portals.

**Limbo / Inside** (Playdead, 2010/2016)
Dark side-scrollers. Move right. Die. Learn. Repeat.
*Does*: moves, dies repeatedly → reads environment to survive.
*Interest*: L1-L2. Physics as primary language. Death as feedback.

**Kentucky Route Zero** (Cardboard Computer, 2013-2020)
Choose what characters *said*, not what they'll say. Choices change tone, not plot.
*Does*: selects dialogue → shapes emotional register, not outcome.
*Interest*: L3 but the branch is interpretive. Choice = authorship of meaning.

**Outer Wilds** (Mobius Digital, 2019)
Solar system in a 22-minute time loop. Knowledge persists, nothing else.
*Does*: explores → learns → dies → loops → applies knowledge.
*Interest*: L3-L4. State is in the player's HEAD. Knowledge as sole progression.

**Baba Is You** (Hempuli, 2019)
Rules are physical objects. Push words to change what things are and do.
*Does*: rearranges rule-words → changes world logic → rewrites reality.
*Interest*: L5. Participant modifies the system itself. Meta-agency.

**What Remains of Edith Finch** (Giant Sparrow, 2017)
Explore a house. Each room = a different interaction mechanic for a different story.
*Does*: enters rooms → each has its own interaction language.
*Interest*: L1 but each step introduces a new interaction model. The "Cannery" sequence (split-stick mundane labor + daydream) is legendary.

**Everything** (David OReilly, 2017)
Be anything — atom, bacterium, horse, continent, galaxy. Scale up/down through orders of magnitude.
*Does*: becomes objects → shifts scale → rules change per scale.
*Interest*: scale-shifting as interaction. Perspective change through embodiment.

**Untitled Goose Game** (House House, 2019)
You're a goose. Honk, grab, flap, terrorize a village.
*Does*: honk, grab, hide, manipulate NPCs through environmental changes.
*Interest*: tiny verb set + reactive NPCs = emergent comedy. Social stealth.

**Getting Over It** (Bennett Foddy, 2017)
Man in a cauldron with a hammer. Climb a mountain. No checkpoints. One slip = catastrophe.
*Does*: swings hammer → climbs → fails → narrator philosophizes about loss.
*Interest*: single tool, complex physics, unforgiving environment. L0 mechanically but L3 emotionally (irreversible loss).

**Pippin Barr — The Artist Is Present** (2011)
Enter MoMA. Wait in line. For hours. Sit with Abramovic.
*Does*: waits. That's it.
*Interest*: L0 provocation. Inaction as agency. Time as material.

**The Graveyard** (Tale of Tales, 2008)
Old woman walks slowly through a graveyard to a bench. Sits. Song plays. Walks back. Might die.
*Does*: walks slowly → sits → walks back.
*Interest*: deliberate removal of agency. Discussion piece: what is "meaningful interaction"?

**Cart Life** (Richard Hofmeier, 2011)
Street vendor sim. Physically perform labor (typing, clicking in sequence). Manage childcare, rent, paperwork.
*Does*: performs tedious labor tasks → manages impossible resource allocation.
*Interest*: tedium-as-empathy. The interface communicates struggle.

**Please Knock on My Door** (Levall Games, 2017)
Daily routine of someone with depression. Choose: shower OR eat. Answer door OR stay in bed.
*Does*: makes impossible energy-budget choices.
*Interest*: resource scarcity as empathy mechanic. Every small choice is agonizing.

**A Slow Year** (Ian Bogost, 2010)
Four "game poems." Contemplation with a single button press.
*Does*: watches, waits, presses one button at the right moment.
*Interest*: minimum viable interaction. Discussion piece.

**Luxuria Superbia** (Tale of Tales, 2013)
Touch/stroke the screen to fill a tunnel with color. Game responds to rhythm and pressure.
*Does*: touches → game responds to how you touch, not just where.
*Interest*: touch quality as core variable. Input velocity and pattern as meaningful.

**Desert Golfing** (Justin Smith, 2014)
Endless procedural golf. No reset. Cumulative score is permanent.
*Does*: drags to aim, releases to shoot. Forever. Irreversibly.
*Interest*: permanent continuity. Every shot counts. No undo.

## Walking Sims / Exploration with Strong Interaction Concepts

**Thirty Flights of Loving** (Blendo Games, 2012)
First-person jump-cut narrative about a heist. Cinematic editing but you're always walking.
*Does*: walks continuously while time/space jump-cuts around you.
*Interest*: cinematic editing in first-person. 15 minutes. Perfect student project scope.

**The Beginner's Guide** (Davey Wreden, 2015)
Walk through unfinished game levels while a narrator tells you about their creator. Narrator may be unreliable.
*Does*: walks and clicks → forms judgment about the narrator's reliability.
*Interest*: mechanical agency low, interpretive agency high.

**A Light in Chorus** (Eliott Johnson & Matt Sheridan, 2016)
World made of point clouds. Shine light into darkness to reveal landscape fragments.
*Does*: looks, moves → world assembles from particles based on attention.
*Interest*: vision as revelation. Looking "creates" the world.

## Sound / Music-Driven Works

**Rez / Rez Infinite** (Tetsuya Mizuguchi, 2001/2016)
Shoot enemies in abstract digital landscapes. Every shot adds a musical element. Combat builds the soundtrack.
*Does*: targets, shoots → each action adds a musical layer in sync.
*Interest*: gameplay-as-music-performance. Player inputs ARE musical inputs. L1-L2.

**Toshio Iwai — Electroplankton** (2005)
Ten species of touchscreen "plankton." Each species = a different input-to-music mapping.
*Does*: touches, draws paths, claps → each species responds differently with music.
*Interest*: ten different interaction models in one work. Each is a self-contained project.

**Jono Brandel & Lullatone — Patatap** (2014)
Press any key. Each key triggers a unique sound + animated shape. Play the keyboard as an audiovisual instrument.
*Does*: presses keys → instant audiovisual feedback → performs.
*Interest*: L0 reaction with emergent depth through combination. Zero learning curve.

**Incredibox** (So Far So Good, 2009-present)
Drag-drop sound icons onto beatboxers. Compose a track by combining layers.
*Does*: drags sounds → composes → certain combos trigger bonus sequences.
*Interest*: constrained composition. Impossible to sound bad, but mastery matters.

**Amit Pitaru — Sonic Wire Sculptor** (2003)
Draw lines in 3D. A playhead sweeps through the drawing. Where it crosses a line, a note sounds. 3D drawing = musical score.
*Does*: draws in 3D → drawing is "played" as music.
*Interest*: spatial position = pitch/timing. Drawing-as-composition.

## Works with Unusual Input

**Eyewriter** (Zach Lieberman, Evan Roth, et al., 2009)
Eye-tracking lets a paralyzed graffiti artist draw with gaze. Drawings projected on buildings.
*Does*: looks → draws → art projected at massive scale.
*Interest*: gaze as drawing tool. Constraint transforms the creative act.

**Bounden** (Game Oven, 2014)
Two players hold opposite ends of one phone. Tilt together to guide a sphere. The required movements make you dance together.
*Does*: tilts shared device → forced into cooperative dance.
*Interest*: shared controller forces embodied cooperation. Phone as choreographer.

**Robin Baumgarten — Line Wobbler** (2014)
Dungeon crawler on a single LED strip. Controller is a spring-mounted door stopper you wobble and snap.
*Does*: wobbles, bends, snaps a spring → navigates 1D world.
*Interest*: physical controller richness vs. minimal visual output. The feel is everything.

**Vectorpark — Windowsill** (2009)
Surreal toy-like scenes. Pull, twist, drag, poke strange objects. Discover what they do.
*Does*: pokes things → discovers interactions through experimentation.
*Interest*: tactile curiosity. No instructions. Each scene is a self-contained interaction surprise.

**Vectorpark — Metamorphabet** (2015)
Each letter is an interactive toy. Poke "A" → grows antlers → antlers become arms → arms become arch.
*Does*: taps/drags → letter cascades through chain of transformations.
*Interest*: gestural chain reactions. Simple input, elaborate surprising response.

## Participatory / Social / Site-Specific

**Blast Theory — Can You See Me Now?** (2001)
Online players navigate a virtual city map. Real runners with GPS chase their avatars on actual streets.
*Does*: moves in virtual space → real humans hunt you in physical space.
*Interest*: mixed reality pursuit. Physical and virtual layered. Golden Nica Ars Electronica.

**Punchdrunk — Sleep No More** (2011)
Wear a mask. Freely roam a massive building where Macbeth unfolds simultaneously across dozens of rooms. Choose who to follow.
*Does*: walks, chooses which rooms to enter, which characters to follow.
*Interest*: spatial narrative choice. You can only be in one place at a time. Your path IS your edit of the story.

**Chris Milk & Aaron Koblin — The Wilderness Downtown** (2010)
Enter your childhood address. Multiple browser windows show Google Street View of YOUR neighborhood. Draw trees planted in your street. Write a letter to your younger self.
*Does*: enters personal data → experience is built from their own geography.
*Interest*: personalization through real data. Your world becomes the material.

## Reinterpretation Candidates

These are primarily visual/spatial — weak on agency as-is, strong starting points for "what if this were interactive?"

**Olafur Eliasson — The Weather Project** (2003) — artificial sun in Tate Turbine Hall. *What if the sun responded to collective movement?*

**Yayoi Kusama — Infinity Mirror Rooms** — reflective environments. *What if movement created or destroyed reflections?*

**James Turrell — light spaces** — perceptual light. *What if light responded to pace, stillness, gaze?*

**Ryoji Ikeda — data installations** — massive data visualizations. *What if participant actions generated the data?*

**Anish Kapoor — Cloud Gate** (2006) — reflective sculpture. *What if the distortion responded to movement?*
