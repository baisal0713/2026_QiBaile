## 1. WOODEN MIRROR — Daniel Rozin (1999)

**Interaction:** Stand in front of 830 motorized wooden tiles — a hidden camera captures your silhouette and each tile tilts independently to create a low-resolution wooden "reflection" of your body in real-time.

### Links & Documentation

| Artsy | https://www.artsy.net/artist/daniel-rozin |
| Wikipedia | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danny_Rozin |

### Video

- bitforms Vimeo (1999 version): https://vimeopro.com/bitforms/rozin/video/7820888
- bitforms Vimeo (2014 version): https://vimeopro.com/bitforms/rozin/video/101408845

### Technical Specs

- 830 wooden tiles + 830 servo motors, octagonal arrangement (35×29 grid)
- Dimensions: 67" × 80" × 10" (170 × 203 × 25 cm)
- Hidden video camera captures visitor, custom software digitizes feed to 35×29 pixel resolution
- Each tile tilts ±30° to modulate reflected light, creating grayscale values
- ~15 fps refresh rate; produces distinctive auditory feedback (hundreds of tiny motors)
- Materials: non-reflective maple tiles, servo motors, computer, custom software

### Exhibition History

- SIGGRAPH 2000 Art Gallery
- Victoria & Albert Museum
- LSU Museum of Art ("Mirror Mirror," Nov 2023 – Mar 2024)
- The Exploratorium, San Francisco
- W Hotel Seoul (permanent)
- Continuously represented by bitforms gallery (New York)

### Artist Context

Daniel Rozin (b. 1961, Jerusalem) — faculty at NYU ITP. His "Mechanical Mirrors" series (1999–present) explores the intersection of digital technology and physical materials. 25+ mirrors made from wood, trash, acrylic, pompoms, steel, RGB lights. Each uses the same principle: computer vision → material actuation → reflection. Awards: Prix Ars Electronica, Chrysler Design Award, Rothschild Prize.

## 2. PULSE ROOM — Rafael Lozano-Hemmer (2006)

**Interaction:** Grip a heart rate sensor — your heartbeat is detected and the closest incandescent bulb flashes in sync with your pulse. Earlier visitors' heartbeats are pushed along a grid of 300 bulbs, creating a collective, time-based visualization of multiple heartbeats.

### Links & Documentation

|----------|-----|
| Artist website | https://www.lozano-hemmer.com/ |
| Project page | https://www.lozano-hemmer.com/pulse_room.php |
| MoMA collection | https://www.moma.org/collection/works/180180 |
| Hirshhorn exhibition | https://hirshhorn.si.edu/exhibitions/rafael-lozano-hemmer-pulse/ |
| MAC Montreal | https://macm.org/en/collections/oeuvre/pulse-room/ |
| bitforms gallery | https://www.bitforms.art/artwork/pulse-room |
| Technical manual (PDF) | https://www.lozano-hemmer.com/texts/manuals/pulse_room.pdf |

### Video

- Venice Biennale 2007: https://vimeopro.com/bitforms/lozano-hemmer/video/33929373
- ARoS Aarhus 2009: https://vimeopro.com/bitforms/lozano-hemmer/video/33927355

### Technical Specs

- 100–300 clear incandescent bulbs (300W each), hung at ~3m in a grid
- Heart rate sensor interface (hand grip) → USB → DMX circuit board → dimmer packs
- Each bulb modulates voltage to flash at visitor's exact heartbeat rhythm
- New participants push earlier recordings along the grid
- Entire room becomes a temporal sequence of multiple heartbeats

### Exhibition History

- 2006: Plataforma 06, Puebla, Mexico (premiere)
- 2007: 52nd Venice Biennale (first artist representing Mexico)
- 2009: ARoS Aarhus Kunstmuseum, Denmark
- 2018: Hirshhorn Museum, Washington D.C.
- Collections: MoMA, Hirshhorn, MAC Montreal, Guggenheim, Tate, SFMOMA, Museo Jumex

### Artist Context

Rafael Lozano-Hemmer (b. 1967, Mexico City) — Mexican-Canadian artist, coined "relational architecture" in 1994. Pulse series includes Pulse Topology, Pulse Park, Pulse Corniche, Pulse Voronoi. Broader practice: biometric installations, large-scale public interventions. Represented by bitforms and Pace Gallery.

## 3. BODY MOVIES — Rafael Lozano-Hemmer (2001)

**Interaction:** Walk across an illuminated public square — your shadow on the façade reveals hidden portraits of strangers from that city. Powerful xenon lamps wash out the projected images; only shadows make them visible. The portraits emerge within your silhouette.

## 6. THE TREACHERY OF SANCTUARY — Chris Milk (2012)

**Interaction:** Stand before a 30-foot triptych above a reflecting pool. Panel 1: your shadow appears and dissolves into birds. Panel 2: birds attack your silhouette. Panel 3: raise your arms — wings grow from your shadow and you fly away.

## 9. LEARNING TO SEE — Memo Akten (2017)

**Interaction:** Arrange physical objects on a tabletop — a camera captures the scene and neural networks trained on specific datasets (oceans, flowers, fire, cosmos) reinterpret your hand movements and objects in real-time, transforming ordinary gestures into alien visual landscapes. ## 10. ZERSEHER (DE-VIEWER) — Joachim Sauter & Dirk Lüsebrink / ART+COM (1992)

**Interaction:** Stand before a framed Renaissance painting — an eye-tracker follows your gaze. Wherever you look, the painting dissolves and distorts in real-time. To see the painting, you must look away. Observation destroys the observed.

### Links & Documentation

| Resource | URL |
|----------|-----|
| Joachim Sauter portfolio | http://www.joachimsauter.com/en/work/zerseher.html |
| ART+COM Studios | https://artcom.de/en/?project=de-viewer |
| Media Art Net | http://www.medienkunstnetz.de/works/der-zerseher/ |
| GitHub (2014 redesign) | https://github.com/artcom/Zerseher |

### Technical Specs

- Framed rear-projection screen displaying digital reproduction of Caroto's *Portrait of a Child with a Drawing* (c. 1558)
- Infrared camera + PC behind screen tracks viewer's iris position
- Custom algorithm distorts image at gaze point in real-time
- Painting regenerates if no viewer looks for 30+ seconds
- Pioneering application of eye-tracking technology (1991–92)

### Exhibition History

- Prix Ars Electronica 1992 — Award of Distinction, Interactive Art
- 2014: Berlin Akademie der Künste, "Schwindel der Wirklichkeit" (redesigned version)

### Artist Context

**Joachim Sauter** (1959–2021) — co-founder of ART+COM (1988, Berlin), Professor at UdK Berlin and UCLA. Created Terravision (1993), the pioneering virtual globe that preceded Google Earth (subject of Netflix's *The Billion Dollar Code*, 2021). **ART+COM** combined artists, designers, scientists, and members of the Chaos Computer Club.

## 11. BOUNDARY FUNCTIONS — Scott Snibbe (1998)

**Interaction:** Walk on a floor projection with other people — an overhead camera tracks positions and generates a real-time Voronoi diagram dividing the floor into personal territories. Only activates with 2+ people. Every step restructures the geometry. Personal space made visible as social mathematics.

### Links & Documentation

| Resource | URL |
|----------|-----|
| Project page | https://www.snibbe.com/art/boundaryfunctions |
| Artist website | https://www.snibbe.com/ |
| MoMA collection | https://www.moma.org/artists/44115 |
| Whitney Museum | https://whitney.org/artists/8448 |
| NTT ICC Tokyo | https://www.ntticc.or.jp/en/archive/works/boundary-functions-2/ |
| Archive of Digital Art | https://digitalartarchive.at/database/general/work/boundary-functions.html |
| Whitney Artport (code) | https://artport.whitney.org/v2/commissions/codedoc/snibbe.shtml |

### Technical Specs

- Overhead camera via mirror mount → real-time computer vision
- Projector onto 4m × 4m floor surface
- Morphological algorithms for noise reduction → contour tracing → Voronoi computation
- 1 person: no response. 2 people: bisecting line. 3+: cellular territories
- Title references Theodore Kaczynski's 1967 PhD thesis on boundary functions

### Exhibition History

- 1998: Ars Electronica (premiere, prize winner)
- NTT ICC, Tokyo (2006–2007)
- Eyebeam, New York; New Langton Arts, San Francisco
- ICA London; The Kitchen, New York
- Milwaukee Art Museum ("Act/React" exhibition)
- Collections: MoMA, Whitney Museum

### Artist Context

Scott Snibbe (b. 1969) — computer scientist + fine artist, pioneer of interactive projection art. Named on six Adobe patents. Later works: Björk's Biophilia app (2011, first app acquired by MoMA), Eyegroove (acquired by Facebook 2016, became Instagram Stories/Reels effects). Published: *How to Train a Happy Mind* (2024, foreword by the Dalai Lama).

## 15. SOUNDSELF: A TECHNODELIC — Robin Arnott (2020)

**Interaction:** Sustain vocal tones into a microphone — the system analyzes pitch, volume, and timbre in real-time, generating synchronous fractals and mandala visuals while binaural audio harmonizes with your voice. A feedback loop forms: sound shapes light, light shapes breath, breath shapes sound. Designed to dissolve self-awareness into meditative flow.

### Technical Specs

- Microphone → real-time audio analysis (pitch, volume, timbre)
- Procedural fractal/mandala visuals respond to vocal characteristics
- Binaural audio engine harmonizes with voice
- Abstract geometries avoid representational imagery (reduces critical thinking)

Robin Arnott — sound designer based in Austin, TX. Previously: sound design for Antichamber (2013); created Deep Sea (2010, audio-only horror with blacked-out gas mask). Coined "technodelic" design — weaving hypnosis, meditation, and neuroscience into interactive feedback loops. Now part of Entheo Digital, focusing on therapeutic applications.
